01 / The brief

What Real actually asked

Design the AI-powered morning command center for a team lead agent, fronting a hundred background skills. On paper it reads as a dashboard problem. It is not.

The team lead agent lives at two altitudes at once. She closes her own deals and she runs a team of twelve. Both demand attention, all day, from the same pair of eyes. Behind her, the AI named Leo runs a hundred skills overnight: listing research, compliance review, negotiation prep, scheduling, lead qualification. The brief was to design the first screen she sees when she wakes up.

The grading rubric told me where the real weight sat. Microinteractions and AI behavior, and agent orchestration, were the heaviest two dimensions. Everything else mattered, but those two were the test.

Microinteractions and AI behaviorheaviest

The constraint underneath every other constraint was not layout. It was trust. The real brief was: design how an agent earns, keeps, and recovers a skeptical human's trust.
02 / The wrong turn

The dashboard dead-end

A hundred agents are running, so show me the hundred agents. I sketched the mission-control grid. The moment I looked at it, I knew I had built the thing she is already drowning in.

I had taken a problem of too much information and answered it with more information. More surfaces, more numbers, more red dots. The dashboard scales anxiety with agent count. The healthier the AI gets, the busier the screen gets. That is exactly backwards.

I answered a too-much-information problem with more information.

So I threw it out and asked a different question. Not what can the agents show me, but what is the one moment where the AI either earns her trust or loses it forever. The answer was the first thirty seconds of her morning. One hand on the phone. She is not browsing. She is bracing.

The other three roads I did not take

DEAD END / B
Two separate tabs

A My-Deals tab and a Team tab. It forces her to mentally diff two lists every morning, the exact context-switch the product should remove. This dead end is what justified the unified signal currency.

DEAD END / C
A single chatbot as the whole UX

Just talk to Leo. One chat bubble cannot express confident, uncertain, or silent at a glance, or hold two altitudes. Conversation became one affordance, not the spine.

DEAD END / D
Raw percentage confidence

An 87 percent confident chip. People misread the precision, and it manufactures false certainty. Switched to categorical confidence with visible unknowns.

03 / The user

Dana Reyes

Every decision after the reframe came from her, not from me.

Dana Reyes
Team Lead Agent
ROLERuns a team of twelve agents inside a brokerage heading toward a hundred and eighty thousand agents after the RE/MAX merger.
TENSIONA producer who became a manager without ever being given a manager's tools. Her own deals and her team's deals fight for the same attention all day.
HISTORYBurned by software before. Every tool promised to help and then cried wolf. She has learned a quiet helplessness, and she does not trust the green checkmark.
For Dana, trust is not the default state. Trust is scarce, and it has to be earned on every single screen.

So the brief was never really design a dashboard. It was design trust. How does an agent talk when it is confident, when it is unsure, when it is wrong, and when it has nothing to say. Get those voices right and Dana relaxes. Get them wrong and she goes back to doing it all in her head.

04 / The reframe

A triage of exceptions

The hundred agents already did the work overnight. The first screen does one thing: surface the few things quietly going wrong, and hand back the decisions only a human should make.

PRINCIPLE 01

Triage, not dashboard

A dashboard answers how is everything. Dana already knows the answer is mostly fine with a few fires. So the screen shows the fires, ranked, about three a morning. The default state is nothing needs you, and that is a designed result, not an empty screen.

PRINCIPLE 02

One signal currency

Every item, her deal or a teammate's, is the same underlying event: money quietly leaking while no one was looking. Rank by what is at stake. Hers and theirs compete in one list, so nothing slips because it was on the wrong tab.

PRINCIPLE 03

Altitude as a badge

Whose deal it is becomes a small badge on the card, not a separate screen. That one move dissolves the two-altitude tension instead of displaying it. A filter she can apply, never the default she has to swivel between.

07 / The five voices

How the AI talks

The heaviest-weighted dimension. Leo has five voices, and each one is a color, a mechanic, and a feeling. Watch the swatch.

Thinking
No spinner. A named shimmer, sized to the answer it is about to give, telling you which skill is working on what. The wait becomes proof of work.
A colleague glancing up to say, give me a second, I am checking.
Confident
Deliberately boring. A calm reveal, one line of reasoning, tappable source chips. No confetti. A confident AI that celebrates is a tool that does not respect your time.
A reliable colleague, not a slot machine.
Uncertain
The type literally gets lighter. Uncertainty becomes visual weight. It gives ranges, not fake precision, says what it is missing, and always offers accept or reject.
Someone honest enough to say, I think so, but please confirm.
Wrong
The design assumes it. No dead end. Acknowledge, correct, escalate if needed, with a timed undo. One correction propagates to every relevant skill at once. One human nudge, a hundred-agent consistency.
A teammate who owns the mistake and fixes it everywhere, fast.
Silent
The one I am proudest of. Silence gets a surface, the Silence Ledger. Handled 47, chose not to interrupt on 6. Tap to see the near-misses. The receipt for restraint.
The quiet of a desk where someone already cleared the small fires.
08 / Orchestration

A hundred into one

A hundred agents cannot each have a voice, or it is chaos. One orchestrator, named skill-workers underneath. Dana only ever talks to Leo.

Underneath Leo sit named skill-workers: Listing Research, Compliance QA, Negotiation Prep, Scheduling, Lead Qualification, Market Analysis. The hundred are drill-down detail. Every piece of the UI maps to one of three disclosure levels.

L1 . GLYPHA status. A colored dot, a state. What is happening, at a glance.
L2 . LINEOne line of why, plus the metric. Why Leo flagged it, in a sentence.
L3 . TRACEThe full trace, drillable to page four of the inspection report, checked by Compliance QA. Where the claim came from.

The notification logic is built to suppress, not surface. Four tiers. Needs-you rises to the top. Handled goes silently into the ledger. Noise never appears.

09 / How I worked

Vibe-coded, honestly logged

This role is about building, not just drawing. Three things about the craft.

Hand-coded, not assembled

The entire prototype is hand-built HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. No page builder. Three complete navigable apps and the live state machine, written and debugged in three days. Vibe-coding is not a shortcut here, it is the medium.

CSS motion, not JS timing

One deliberate engineering call: every animation lives in CSS keyframes and transitions, never JavaScript timing loops. Content is fully visible by default; motion is pure enhancement. It survives a strict content-security-policy and degrades gracefully. That is the design-engineering bridge the role asks for.

10 / Next week

If I had another week

I would not add features. The scarcest resource here is not screens. It is calibrated trust, and you only earn that with real users.

SURFACE 02

The desktop war room

The same signal currency at a different altitude. The mobile triage is the decision surface; the desktop is where side-by-side density and the altitude handoff live. Start triage on the phone, drop into the deal on the desktop with no context reload.

CALIBRATION

Real-user calibration

Run this in front of three real team leads and tune the escalation threshold against their actual mornings. The line between needs-you and handled is hand-tuned today; it needs behavioral data. And test how rarely the Silence Ledger should surface before it reads as noise instead of trust.

The close

A hundred agents worked all night so Dana could look at her phone for thirty seconds, see the three things that matter, trust the rest was handled, and put the phone down. The agents are not the product. The calm is.
